About The Mechanic

Software reviews for the rest of us.

Desk Work Dan exists for one reason: to help regular working businesses find software that actually works — without overpaying or getting buried in Silicon Valley jargon.

Why this site exists

Walk into any plumbing shop, roofing company, mechanic's garage, or landscaping outfit in America and you'll see the same thing: smart, hard-working people getting talked down to by software companies that don't understand their business.

The pricing is confusing. The websites are full of buzzwords. The "free demos" are sales calls. And every review you find online is either written by an intern who never used the product, or by an "expert" pushing whatever pays the highest commission.

We started Desk Work Dan because somebody needs to translate. The tools really can save you hours every week and thousands of dollars a year — if you pick the right one. We help you do that.

The Desk Work Dan Promise

Four things we'll always do, and four things we'll never do.

We will…
  • · Pay full price for every product we review.
  • · Use it on real jobs for at least 30 days.
  • · Tell you who shouldn't buy it.
  • · Write everything in plain English.
We will not…
  • · Take payment to bump up a score.
  • · Recommend a tool we wouldn't use ourselves.
  • · Hide which links earn us money.
  • · Use the words "synergy," "platform," or "leverage."

Who is The Mechanic?

M

The Mechanic is the persona behind every review on this site. Think of him as the buddy at the parts counter who has actually run a small business, dealt with stack of receipts at the end of the year, and wasted his own money on software that didn't work out.

He's spent the better part of two decades around shops, trucks, and back offices. He's been the guy answering the phone, the guy invoicing on Sunday night, and the guy explaining to dad why the new "system" is faster than the wall calendar.

When you read a review here, that's the voice you're getting. No marketing department. No "growth team." Just somebody who has lived the problem and has finally found a few tools worth recommending.

How we keep the lights on

We're upfront about it: a lot of the links on this site are affiliate links. If you click through and end up buying a product, the company sometimes pays us a small commission. You don't pay a penny extra — the cost is the same whether you use our link or type the URL yourself.

That's how a small site like this stays free, ad-free, and free of pop-ups. Just so we're crystal clear: commissions never change our recommendation. If a tool stinks, we say so — even if they're paying the highest rate in the industry. We'd rather lose a sale than lose your trust.
